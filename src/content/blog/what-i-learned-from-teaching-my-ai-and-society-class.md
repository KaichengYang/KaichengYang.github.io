## TL;DR

- I taught [AI and Society](https://yangkclab.github.io/ai-and-society-course/Spring2026/) as a seminar for 19 CS grad students --- my first new course as a new faculty member.
- Letting students use AI freely (with full transparency) shifted assessment toward in-class participation and project presentations.
- Hallucinated references in assignments became a teachable moment.
- Access to paid AI models created an equity gap I ended up bridging with my own research funds.
- I plan to keep offering the course; reach out if you have suggestions.

## A new course

I recently wrapped up my [AI and Society course](https://yangkclab.github.io/ai-and-society-course/Spring2026/) for the spring semester.
This is the first time I taught this course, and I feel very lucky it went smoothly for most of it, which is more than anyone can hope for with a brand-new course.
Here I'm sharing my reflections on it.

As a background, I joined Binghamton University last fall and pretty much scrambled through my whole first year (the summer has finally arrived!).
Around the middle of the fall semester, the department began preparing the spring course schedule.
That's when I decided to propose a new course because I didn't want to be assigned classes that weren't very relevant to my research.

I knew I wanted to teach something at the intersection of AI and society, which is what I study and am deeply interested in.
I also knew I didn't have the capacity to prepare a lecture-based course within the time frame.
So eventually I decided to propose a seminar-style course in which, for most of the semester, the students and I would read and discuss papers (and other materials).
The course has a semester-long project in which students can choose any subject they want, as long as it's about AI and relevant to the course topics (which is pretty much everything).
Students also needed to write an essay reflecting on how AI had changed their lives and work by the end of the semester.

I want to give a shout-out to my department here because they were very supportive throughout the process, especially since I heard that some junior faculty members didn't have the flexibility to propose and teach new courses at other institutions.
My course proposal got approved later that semester, and I needed to build a new course from scratch!
I was only able to create a skeleton of the course before spring began, with a rough plan of the topics we'd cover each week.
Then I pretty much had to finalize the course materials on the fly.
The upside was that I could include very recent materials, and it surprised me how fast the field evolved even within a single semester.

From the beginning, I knew I wanted to share as much as possible with the public.
So I built a [website](https://yangkclab.github.io/ai-and-society-course/Spring2026/) for the course, where you can find the [syllabus](https://yangkclab.github.io/ai-and-society-course/versions/Spring2026/content/syllabus.pdf) and the [reading list](https://yangkclab.github.io/ai-and-society-course/Spring2026/readings).
Some students gave permission for their essays to be shared publicly, and you can find them [here](https://yangkclab.github.io/ai-and-society-course/Spring2026/essays).

## Teaching CS students a non-typical CS course

There were 19 students in my class this spring, most of whom were master's students, and a few were doctoral students from CS.
Now that the semester has ended, I want to thank them for their trust in me (someone who just started teaching and proposed this new course).

In the first class, I told the students that this would not be a typical CS course.
I wouldn't be teaching them any algorithms, data structures, or programming languages.
Instead, we would be reading a lot of materials, including research papers, industry white papers, policy documents, and even blogs and social media posts.
And we would be discussing those materials in class.

Moreover, the topics would be very diverse, ranging from science production to health and governance---topics that typically wouldn't come up in other CS classes.
My argument, which I also made in class, is that AI is set to affect every aspect of our lives and society, and since AI is a topic CS majors care about, it makes sense for them to learn how it plays out in other fields.
That said, I had no idea what would happen when I asked students to read papers on using AI to facilitate synthetic chemistry or how doctors are adopting AI in practice.

To that end, I think expectations are critical.
My goal was simple: I wanted students to gain a sense of how AI is used across different disciplines and contexts, prompting them to think outside the CS box.
They wouldn't magically become domain experts after the semester, and the knowledge they picked up might quickly expire given how fast technology evolves (which is actually one of the points I wanted to make in the course).
But at least I hoped they could start thinking about how AI is changing the world around them, ask questions, and seek answers on their own.

## Student engagement

As a seminar-style course, in-class participation is the most important aspect.
But I was pretty nervous about it because I was facing a room of CS majors who, in my experience, are not the most talkative people.
In fact, I had never taken a similar class myself before coming to the US for my PhD.
I still remember the first seminar-style class I attended.
It was challenging both because I wasn't familiar with the format and because English wasn't my mother tongue.
I believe at least some of the students in my class felt the same way.

I would be lying if I said I had a full house every time, and students were enthusiastically engaged in discussion throughout the semester.
People would miss class, and students would focus on their laptops.
I went to grad school myself, and I know how it goes.
From time to time, I had to use some tricks to get them to show up and talk.

But I'm pretty happy with the attendance and with how the students eventually spoke up in class.
I can see that they had a genuine interest in at least some of the topics.
When they actually shared something, I could feel that they had read the materials and put some thought into them, which is exactly what I hoped for.
It was also encouraging to see shy students start sharing their thoughts with more confidence later in the semester.

## AI policy

One thing worth mentioning is the AI policy for my course.
I know there are a lot of debates about it, and I understand that using AI prematurely could hurt learning outcomes.
But for this class, it wouldn't make sense to ban AI for two main reasons.
First, it's a class about AI, and the goal is to give students firsthand experience using it.
Second, they would probably use it regardless of policy.
So I encouraged students to use AI whenever they could, as long as they were fully transparent about it.
This included using AI to facilitate reading papers and completing their final projects.
It also allowed me to assign materials that would otherwise be quite challenging, such as papers on materials science and lengthy policy documents.

But evaluating learning outcomes became harder as a result.
This is why in-class participation is crucial for the course.
For the reading assignment, I asked students to write a short reflection before class.
But that wasn't all. I'd also ask them to share their thoughts again in class.
They could use AI to summarize a paper and write the reflection, but to say something during class, they would at least have to have read the AI's summary.
That's my goal.

For the course project, students had opportunities to present their proposals and final results, where I would ask clarifying questions and give feedback.
Again, they could use AI to produce project results, but they'd need a solid understanding of the work to present and answer questions.
In short, these in-person interactions are how I kept them engaged and ensured they were actually learning.
One downside is that this approach doesn't scale.
For my class, 12--15 students might be the optimal size for thorough interactions with everyone.
If I were facing 40 students, this wouldn't work.

Another thing I wanted to teach was how to use AI effectively and responsibly.
And the most important lesson is that they should never blindly trust AI output, and validation is always necessary.
That said, there aren't many ways to detect irresponsible AI use (I don't trust those AI detectors).
Perhaps the most telling indicator is hallucinated references, which I mentioned several times in class.
And yet some students still submitted assignments with hallucinated references.

I know some professors would treat this as academic dishonesty.
But things are tricky in my case because I was the one who allowed and even encouraged them to use AI, and to some extent, this outcome was expected.
Plus, based on my interactions with students, I do believe they put considerable effort into their assignments, though they got lazy with references.
So what I did was have one-on-one conversations with these students and use them as an opportunity to teach them the consequences of blindly trusting AI.
I'd like to think they'll remember this more vividly and be more careful with AI outputs going forward.

## A new digital divide

Another challenge was a new digital divide.
For this course, I think it's essential that students get hands-on experience with state-of-the-art AI models.
Unfortunately, these aren't free.
Some universities provide access to ChatGPT or Claude for all students, which is commendable.
Our university currently only has a deal with Google, but Gemini is, you know, Gemini.
As a result, students who wanted to use ChatGPT or Claude had to pay for subscriptions themselves, and not everyone can afford that.
This unequal access to AI models likely led to different learning outcomes in my class, but there is not much I can do here.

For the course project, many students' proposed studies involved interacting with the APIs of various AI models, which are not free either.
Unfortunately, we didn't have dedicated funding to support the students and the course yet.
After talking with some of the students facing this challenge, I decided to cover their costs with my own research funds.
I don't think this is how it should work, and it's not a sustainable solution, but at least I made sure the students in my class got the experience they needed.
On a related note: if you ever have to run something similar, I'd recommend [OpenRouter](https://openrouter.ai).
It lets you access all kinds of AI models and makes it easy to create individual API keys with limited credits and expiration dates that you can easily share with the students.

## Where to go from here

I plan to keep offering this course.
The field evolves so fast that I'll need to update the content substantially every iteration, but I think that's actually good for keeping me current.
If you have any suggestions about the reading materials or how I teach the course, please feel free to reach out.
